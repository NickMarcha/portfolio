# Signs of AI Writing — Avoid These Patterns

Condensed from Wikipedia's field guide. Apply as rules when writing significant text.

---

## Content

### Undue emphasis on significance, legacy, broader trends
- Avoid puffing up importance with phrases like: *stands/serves as*, *is a testament/reminder*, *a vital/significant/crucial/pivotal/key role/moment*, *underscores/highlights its importance*, *reflects broader*, *symbolizing its ongoing/enduring/lasting*, *contributing to the*, *setting the stage for*, *marking/shaping the*, *represents/marks a shift*, *key turning point*, *evolving landscape*, *focal point*, *indelible mark*, *deeply rooted*
- Don't add statements about how mundane topics "represent broader movements" or "contribute to history"
- Don't over-emphasize ecosystem connections, conservation status, or preservation efforts when they're generic or tenuous

### Undue emphasis on notability and media coverage
- Avoid: *independent coverage*, *local/regional/national media outlets*, *profiled in*, *written by a leading expert*, *active social media presence*, *maintains a strong digital presence*
- Don't list sources as proof of notability without saying what they actually said
- Don't attribute trivial or uncontroversial facts to named sources in body text

### Superficial analyses
- Avoid attaching "-ing" phrases that add vague significance: *highlighting/underscoring/emphasizing*, *ensuring*, *reflecting/symbolizing*, *contributing to*, *cultivating/fostering*, *encompassing*, *valuable insights*, *align/resonate with*
- Don't add analysis of "significance" or "impact" that isn't grounded in specific facts

### Promotional and advertisement-like language
- Avoid: *boasts a*, *vibrant*, *rich* (figurative), *profound*, *enhancing*, *showcasing*, *exemplifies*, *commitment to*, *natural beauty*, *nestled*, *in the heart of*, *groundbreaking*, *renowned*, *featuring*, *diverse array*
- Keep a neutral tone; avoid travel-guide or press-release style

### Vague attributions
- Avoid: *Industry reports*, *Observers have cited*, *Experts argue*, *Some critics argue*, *several sources* (when only few are cited), *such as* (before exhaustive lists)
- Don't attribute opinions to vague authorities
- Don't exaggerate how many sources hold a view

### Outline-like conclusions
- Avoid rigid "Despite its success, X faces challenges..." followed by vague positive assessment or "Future Prospects" speculation
- Don't use formulaic "Challenges and Legacy" or "Future Outlook" sections

### Other content patterns
- Don't treat list or broad article titles as proper nouns in leads (e.g. "The List of X refers to...")
- Don't fill "see also" with broad, generic terms

## Language and grammar

### AI vocabulary — use sparingly or avoid
- *Additionally* (especially sentence-start)
- *align with*, *boasts*, *bolstered*, *crucial*, *delve*, *emphasizing*, *enduring*, *enhance*, *fostering*, *garner*, *highlight* (verb), *interplay*, *intricate/intricacies*, *key* (adjective), *landscape* (abstract), *meticulous/meticulously*, *pivotal*, *showcase*, *tapestry* (abstract), *testament*, *underscore* (verb), *valuable*, *vibrant*

### Avoid substituting simple copulatives
- Prefer *is/are* over: *serves as*, *stands as*, *marks*, *represents*, *boasts*, *features*, *offers*
- Prefer *has* over *features* or *offers*

### Negative parallelisms
- Avoid "Not only X but also Y" and "It's not just X, it's Y" constructions when they feel like clearing up imagined misconceptions
- Avoid "Not X, but Y" and "no X, no Y, just Z" patterns that sound like retroactive corrections

### Rule of three
- Don't overuse "adjective, adjective, adjective" or "phrase, phrase, and phrase" structures

### Elegant variation
- Don't avoid repeating a word by cycling through synonyms (protagonist, key player, eponymous character) — repetition is often clearer

## Style

### Headings
- Don't capitalize all main words in headings (avoid Title Case for section headers)

### Emphasis
- Don't overuse boldface; avoid "key takeaways" style where every instance of a term is bolded

### Lists
- Avoid inline-header vertical lists: "**Term:** description" as bullet format
- Prefer prose where it reads naturally

### Punctuation and formatting
- Don't overuse em dashes (—); use commas, parentheses, or colons where appropriate
- Avoid emoji in headings or bullet points
- Use straight quotes (") not curly (“ ”) unless required by style

### Structure
- Don't skip heading levels (e.g. going from h1 to h3)
- Don't create unnecessary small tables when prose would work

## Communication and meta

### Collaborative filler
- Avoid: *I hope this helps*, *Of course!*, *Certainly!*, *You're absolutely right!*, *Would you like...*, *is there anything else*, *let me know*, *more detailed breakdown*, *here is a*
- Don't include text meant for the user (e.g. "In this section we will discuss...") in the final output

### Knowledge-cutoff disclaimers
- Avoid: *as of my last knowledge update*, *While specific details are limited/scarce*, *not widely available/documented*, *based on available information*, *keeps personal life private*
- Don't speculate about gaps or add disclaimers about incomplete information

### Phrasal templates and placeholders
- Don't leave [bracketed placeholders] or fill-in-the-blank text
- Don't use subject lines like "Subject: Request for..."

---

## Summary

Write specifically. Prefer simple words and structures. Avoid puffery, vague attribution, and formulaic patterns. When in doubt, use *is*, *has*, and concrete facts instead of significance-fluff.
